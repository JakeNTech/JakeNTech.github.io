A-Level Project

This is a page dedicated to my A-Level Project, trust me it needs a whole page! The Duck is just too needy!

Context

Before you read on anymore it might be wise for me to give you a bit of context into the background picture and what you are (probably) about to read. Our year 12/13 computer science class had a bit of a thing for ducks (NOT IT THAT WAY!), it came around after one of the two teachers told us about rubber duck debugging, and how when the other teacher teaches us about it she would likely give us some squeaky ducks to help us with our project. We where then told how annoying said ducks where; so, we all have the great idea to hide ducks into our A-Level projects. (I hadn’t thought of the duck at this point).

"What can possibly go wrong?"

When we had to come up with an idea, I was going to make a speed sign detection thing, as I found my parents asking me what the speed limit was annoying. But the issue with this was that the code would have only been around 50 lines long so wasn’t complex enough, and as I lack the skills to re-code OpenCV I had to come up with something else. My next thought was a self-driving car; although my dad has his reservations about letting me loose on his car (Especially as I could not drive at the time). Scaling it down bought me to a little tikes car, but this would be a lot of work, as a steering rack would have to be added, some form of golf cart motor and the hole for your feet filled in. It turns out while second-hand little tikes cars are cheap the golf cart motors are expensive and the scrapyard that I know about is around 80 miles away from my house. Although the biggest issue would be how would I get such a car 3 miles to school? I don’t think people would take too kindly to a 6-foot year 13 thundering down the road in a car(ish) where the brake pads are your shoe soles; it would be fun but could get a little precarious!

“I can actually imagine you trying to get into one of those things and attempting to get it here!”
“What do you mean attempting, it would be great…although parking near here could be a bit of a nightmare!”

After having had the meeting where I have to explain my idea to the teachers, It was supposed to be refining ideas and working out what would meet the criteria while still being feasible however it ended up in a lot of laughter about how ridiculous this project was going to be!

You see it wasn’t until I was looking on Amazon for a suitable RC car did, I think of looking for a duck. Unfortunately, amazon didn’t yield any results for me, so I moved over to eBay, where I found the only one…I reached for my card! When it turned up it was bigger than expected, had RGB (ish) lighting and played twinkle twinkle little star repeatedly. After having some fun with it, I stripped out the internals and started to plan how I was going to cram all the hardware into what became a relatively small space was now.

"As it turns out reading the product page is always a good idea rather then just looking at the pictures"

The duck

After having bought two servos and a controller HAT for my Raspberry Pi, it turns out that the servos didn’t do more then 180Degrees (It turns out what I wanted was motors) and a HAT that only worked with those servos! I then bought the correct HAT and fixing the damaged wires on the old motors, I got out the hot glue gun and began the surgery.

After a few weeks, some minor soldering iron burns, some hot glue burns on my leg (Shorts, hot Glue and sitting on the floor don’t mix apparently), a lot of cutting bits of plastic on the garage floor with a torch in between my teeth later and it was all in.

When I started to code, I got a bit lost and then turned to the interface first, a simple HTML page where the user would see a video stream the four bog standard controls and a toggle for self-driving. It was while “surfing the net” I came across this guide that I was able to continue, it turns out that someone else had done what I wanted to achieve, giving me an example to work from. This amazing resource allowed me to continue.

After a lot of troubleshooting and time compiling various python and C modules, it moved. And with some fine tuning was fully functional. Not Amazing but it worked. I planned to test it on the floor of the sports hall but for one reason or another I was unable to, even though we had the room booked (This was not an issue with the duck).

But I was able to test it on the kitchen floor. It was at this point I found out that the duck had a few issues; The first was with stability, the duck had a very heavy backend and too much torque so it wheelied every time it pulled away from the line, oops. The second issue was with the steering, its turning circle was huge, and I mean huge. The last issue was with the battery setup, while the , £14.99 battery bank was able to run the pi for a long amount of time (It doesn’t even have the capacity to give my S5 a full charge) but the 9V battery for the motor was the issue, after one run with the duck it had gone flat, and the good ones are expensive to buy! With all this said it did work!

"I had to saw a bit of wood on the garage floor in the dark, with a torch in my mouth, its OK though I took all the health and safety precautions.... I put my glasses on so I could see what I was doing"

The next item on the agenda I needed to tackle was the documentation, that was a headache, I also think I have blead the world dry of printer toner and paper! As I am yet to find out my final mark Ill refrain from putting it here.

The duck was certainly a fun project that gave me, and probably others, a fair wedge of entertainment, no matter how injured I became due to not really paying attention when using a soldering iron or a coping saw. Having completed the project with the duck it was repurposed into a home server, as it could now be placed near the end of an Ethernet cable while not being an obvious computer. I may rebuild it at some point as the only thing that I took out was the HAT.
