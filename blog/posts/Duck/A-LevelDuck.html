A-Level Project

This is a page dedicated to my A-Level Project, trust me it needs a whole page! The Duck is just too needy!

JakeNTech, June 2019

Before reading on it might be wise for me to fill in a bit of context. For our Computer Science A2 we needed to come up with a programming project, which would make up a small percentage of our grade. Its also worth mentioning that our class had a bit of a thing about ducks, we should never have been told about rubber duck decoding! When it came time to think up of an idea for what we would like to do my idea was for a speed sign detection AI, having just been on a trip to the south coast it annoyed me when, for the most of the way, the person driving kept asking me if I knew what the speed limit was. However, after looking into this it would have been too simplistic, the main script was about 50 lines long due to the reliance on libraries and modules. I wanted to have some form of AI, that is when it hit me…what about a self-driving car? How hard could it be? Bafflingly my parents didn’t seem all too keen for me to do this to their cars, don’t know why. We had to think smaller, some research later showed that there are many little tikes cars online, but then I saw the cost of a golf cart motor, it was a lot of money. It would have also taken a huge amount of work to make steering and breaks. While it would have been amazing it was a bit impractical.

It was required that we had a meeting with our teachers about our project, as I had forgotten I needed to think of something else, I told them about this. The first reaction was how I was actually going to get it the 3 miles to school, to which I said I would “drive” if of course. Can’t help but think people wouldn’t take too kindly to a 6-foot year 13 thundering down the road in a car(ish) where the brake pads are your shoe soles; it would be fun but could get a little precarious! A mobility scooter was also suggested, but none of us realised how well they hold onto their value. While it may have been a bit less disastrous then a whole car, it would be hilarious watching a self-driving mobility scouter tanking through the town centre. A lot of laughter pursued, but it was then suggested to do an RC car instead. Going away and looking on eBay a RC toy duck presented itself, with the funniest listing ever, so naturally my card came out. It had RGB, played music and quacked!

“I can actually imagine you trying to get into one of those things and attempting to get it here!” “What do you mean attempting, it would be great…although parking near here could be a bit of a nightmare!”

Amazingly it arrived too, so the internals where stripped out to make room for some new hardware in what was now a relatively small space. Doing a bit of research, we needed two servos and a controller HAT for the Raspberry Pi. This is where my limited knowledge started to show, the servos where only capable of going 180 degrees, fine for the steering but not so good for moving the duck forward. Why then could the original motors not be used instead, perhaps wiring them up to the HAT or purchasing another one would do the trick. It took one and a half months for the new HAT to arrive, although I suspect it was in Customs for a fair chunk of time!

After testing the new HAT and planning out how the components where to fit in the duck it was time to get it all in. I had some bits of plastic left over from some GCSE Graphics coursework, all the tools I could find in our garage at my disposal and my cheap soldering iron. There was some minor injury, apparently using a hot glue gun while sat on the floor with shorts on isn’t the best idea; Although the best moment had to be cutting a bit of balsa wood on the garage floor in the dark with a torch in my mouth, wood in my feet, coping saw in one hand and a camera in the other, it didn’t go to badly wrong!

"I had to saw a bit of wood on the garage floor in the dark, with a torch in my mouth, its OK though I took all the health and safety precautions.... I put my glasses on so I could see what I was doing"

The duck was now ready for some code to make it work. Python was the language of choice, plenty of modules and libraries to help me out! Quite quickly though I got a lost; Turning my attention to the interface first, a simple HTML page where the user would see a video stream with five buttons for driving and self-driving toggle. Once this was done and it looked alright I had to go back to the rest of the code, while trying to find an answer to a question I came across a blog post in which the creator had already done what I was trying to achieve, although for a much more sophisticated RC car. This gave me an example to work from, it was infinitely helpful and is what aided me to carry on. This blog post can be found here.

It took an age to troubleshoot, a whole weekend was devoted to trying to find out the cause of an error about the model file. Because I didn’t have the resources to train my own model file I used one that someone else had trained; I was using Python3, the model was trained using Python2 version of all the libraries I was using. Installing the needed modules was a pain for both python2 and 3. While they where on PIP they did not support ARM, so they needed to be compiled for ARM. I believe that C played a big part in some of those modules, for better optimization. After this was sorted it worked, a bit.

To this day I am unsure what that model file was trained to do, all it seemed to do was make the duck go in a straight line and that is it. A mate suggested trying to see if it would follow a line of paper, we planned to try this with one of our teachers was watching, as they had to see the final product. I think most teachers where confused when two computing and a couple of science/engineering students sauntered into school, one of which was clutching onto a duck. However, the PE department was more confused as to why this group of mates had booked the sports hall! Unfortunately, the computing teacher could not make it and we were not allowed in unless there was a teacher responsible for us, in case one of fell over or something. The news of the bloke with the duck moved fast and a few people could be overheard talking about it in the corridors the next few days! I remember having a conversation with the head of English, head of Sixth form and my tutor about what the duck.

I was able to test it on the kitchen floor at home though. It was at this point I found that a few issues where raised; The first was with stability, the duck had a very heavy backend with too much torque so it wheelied every time it moved off. The second issue was with the steering, its turning circle was huge, and I mean huge. The last issue was with the battery setup, while the £14.99 battery bank was able to run the pi for a long amount of time (It doesn’t even have the capacity to give my S5 a full charge) but the 9V battery for the motor was the issue, after one run with the duck it had gone flat, and the good ones are expensive to buy! The wheeling issues was solved by blue tacking a spudger to the bottom of the duck; the battery issue was sorted by asking my mum to buy the batteries next time she went to the supermarket. IT LIVED!

The next item on the agenda I needed to tackle was the documentation, that was a headache, I also think I have used the world dry of printer toner and paper, there were far too many pages (Around 200 I recall), It took forever to print and did run the printer out of toner. Hence why it was suggested that mine to be printed out first to mark so it broke every printer in school before anyone else got the chance!

The duck was certainly a fun project that gave me, and probably others, a fair wedge of entertainment for a couple of months, no matter how injured I became due to not really paying attention when using a soldering iron or a coping saw. Having completed the project with the duck it was turned into a HTPC, but I do intend on re building it again one day!
